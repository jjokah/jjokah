## Why Predicting AI Is Hard (and Why You Should Try Anyway)

In 2020, experts widely agreed that AI would not be able to write coherent long-form essays for at least five more years. In November 2022, ChatGPT made that prediction obsolete on its first day.

AI progress is non-linear. It tends to plateau for long periods, then jump dramatically when a new architectural insight, a new scale of compute, or a new training technique unlocks a step change in capability.

This makes confident long-term predictions unreliable. But it does not mean the future is unknowable. Several trends are already in motion, and understanding them is how you make good decisions about your skills, your organization, and your career.

## The AGI Question

### Defining the Terms

**Narrow AI (ANI, Artificial Narrow Intelligence)**
What we have today. Systems that are superhuman at specific tasks, such as playing chess, generating text, and recognizing images, but cannot generalize outside their training domain. A language model that writes brilliantly has no ability to drive a car or understand that fire is hot unless trained on that data.

**Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)**
A hypothetical AI that can learn and perform any intellectual task that a human can, and generalize across entirely new domains without task-specific training. No such system exists today.

**Artificial Superintelligence (ASI)**
A hypothetical AI that surpasses human intelligence across every domain, including the ability to improve itself. A theoretical concept with no existing examples.

### Where the Debate Stands

AI leaders are publicly divided:

- Some researchers (including Demis Hassabis of Google DeepMind and Dario Amodei of Anthropic) believe AGI-level systems could be years, not decades, away
- Many researchers believe current architectural approaches (scaling transformers) will hit fundamental limits before reaching AGI
- Others argue the concept of "general intelligence" is poorly defined and AGI is not a useful target

> **Key Insight:** You don't need to take a position on AGI to be an effective AI practitioner. What matters is tracking the capabilities of systems available today and in the next 1–2 years; that's where actual decisions get made.

## The Four Trends That Define AI's Near Future

### 1. The Agentic Turn

The most significant shift underway is the move from AI as a *responder* to AI as an *actor*.

Current AI systems answer questions and generate content when prompted. **Agentic AI systems** take sequences of actions autonomously: searching the web, writing and executing code, calling APIs, managing files, and completing multi-step tasks without moment-to-moment human direction.

Examples already deployed:
- AI coding agents that autonomously navigate a codebase, implement a feature, write tests, and open a pull request
- AI research agents that autonomously gather data from multiple sources, synthesize findings, and produce a report
- AI customer service agents that handle end-to-end resolution of support tickets across multiple systems

This is the technology that most directly changes what "knowledge work" means. The work humans do shifts from execution to supervision, judgment, and goal-setting.

---

### 2. Multimodality Becomes the Default

The early AI era was text-in, text-out. The current era is text, images, audio, and video, in and out, often in the same interaction.

Models like GPT-4o, Gemini 1.5 Pro, and Claude 3.5 can now:
- Watch a video and answer questions about it
- Listen to audio and transcribe or summarize it
- Look at a document with charts and images and discuss its contents
- Generate responses in audio, not just text

The practical implication: AI tools are becoming natural interaction partners. Voice-first AI interfaces are already here (Advanced Voice Mode in ChatGPT, Gemini Live). The keyboard and text box will become just one of many ways to work with AI.

---

### 3. The Democratization of Model Access

Two years ago, deploying a capable AI model required a team of ML engineers and significant cloud infrastructure. Today:

- **API access**: Any developer can call frontier models via API for fractions of a cent per query
- **Open-source models**: Meta's Llama 3 and Mistral's models run on consumer hardware and match 2022-era frontier model performance
- **Local deployment**: Tools like Ollama let anyone run capable models entirely offline on a laptop
- **No-code platforms**: Bolt.new, Lovable, and Replit Agent let non-engineers build functional applications by describing them

The result: AI capability that once required a research team is now accessible to anyone with a laptop and a clear question.

---

### 4. The Commoditization of Intelligence

As model capabilities converge and API pricing falls, raw AI intelligence is becoming a commodity. The competitive differentiation is shifting to:

- **Data**: Organizations with proprietary data that can be used to customize models have a lasting advantage
- **Integration**: How deeply AI is embedded into workflows, systems, and customer experiences
- **Trust**: Which organizations have earned user trust for AI-assisted decisions
- **Speed**: Which teams can iterate and deploy AI applications fastest

The implication for individuals: the raw ability to "use AI" will become table stakes. The advantage goes to those who combine AI fluency with deep domain expertise and the judgment to know when AI is right and when it is wrong.

## The Regulatory Landscape

Governments are moving to regulate AI, at varying speeds and with different approaches.

### EU AI Act (2024)

The world's first comprehensive AI regulation. Key provisions:

- **Risk-based framework**: AI systems are classified by risk level (unacceptable, high, limited, minimal)
- **Banned applications**: Real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces, social scoring systems, AI that exploits psychological vulnerabilities
- **High-risk requirements**: Systems used in hiring, credit, education, law enforcement, and critical infrastructure face strict requirements for transparency, human oversight, and technical documentation
- **General-purpose AI models**: Foundation models (like GPT-4 or Gemini) face their own transparency and safety requirements
- **Penalties**: Up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover for violations

**What this means for organizations**: If you operate in or serve the EU market, AI systems used in hiring, credit decisions, or customer-facing applications may face compliance requirements. Legal and compliance teams need to be involved in AI deployments.

### United States

The US approach is more fragmented: sector-specific guidance rather than comprehensive legislation as of 2025:

- **Executive Order on AI (2023)**: Required safety testing and reporting for frontier models; established AI safety standards for federal agencies
- **NIST AI Risk Management Framework**: Voluntary guidance for managing AI risk in organizations
- **Sector regulators**: FDA (healthcare AI), SEC (financial AI), FTC (consumer protection) are issuing AI-specific guidance within their domains
- **State laws**: Several states (California, Texas, Colorado) have passed or proposed AI-specific legislation

### Global Picture

- **China**: Requires AI content generated for Chinese users to be watermarked; generative AI services must register with the government
- **UK**: A "pro-innovation" approach with existing regulators applying their frameworks to AI, rather than new legislation
- **International coordination**: G7 nations have agreed to AI governance principles (Hiroshima Process); coordination on frontier AI safety is ongoing

## AI and the Labor Market

### The Honest Assessment

AI will automate some tasks. It will also create new tasks and roles. History suggests technology-driven displacement is real and unevenly distributed; the people hurt by automation are often not the same people who benefit from new roles created.

**Tasks most at risk of automation:**
- High-volume, routine cognitive work: data entry, basic report generation, template-based writing
- Pattern recognition tasks: quality inspection, fraud flagging, medical imaging analysis (as an augmentation tool)
- Process-following: customer service for routine inquiries, basic legal document review

**Tasks most resistant to automation:**
- Complex, contextual judgment: executive decisions, ethical assessments, novel problem-solving
- Physical dexterity in unstructured environments: skilled trades, healthcare procedures
- Deep interpersonal work: therapy, negotiation, teaching in high-stakes contexts, complex sales
- Creative direction: specifying what should be created, not just executing the creation

**New roles AI is creating:**
- Prompt engineers and AI integration specialists
- AI trainers and evaluators (human feedback for model improvement)
- AI governance and compliance officers
- AI product managers
- Domain experts who work alongside and supervise AI systems

> **Key Insight:** The most durable professional strategy is not to compete with AI at tasks AI does well, but to develop skills in judgment, creativity, relationship-building, and domain expertise, and learn to direct and validate AI in your domain.

## How to Stay Current

The half-life of specific AI knowledge is short. The ability to keep learning is the most important long-term skill.

### A Practical System

**Weekly (15–30 minutes):**
- One AI newsletter: *The Rundown AI*, *TLDR AI*, or *Import AI* by Jack Clark
- Skim announcements from the major labs (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Meta AI)

**Monthly (1–2 hours):**
- Try one new AI tool or feature with a real work task
- Read one deeper analysis piece (Ben Evans, Ethan Mollick's *One Useful Thing* blog, or similar)

**Quarterly:**
- Audit your personal AI toolkit: which tools are you using? What's improved? What should you replace?
- Assess your professional skills: what do you do that AI is getting better at? What are you developing to stay ahead?

**When evaluating a new AI claim:**
1. Who is making it? (Lab promoting its own product? Independent researcher? Journalist?)
2. Is there a reproducible benchmark, or just a demo?
3. What are the failure modes and limitations?
4. What would change about my work if this is real?

## Hands-On Practice

### Exercise 1: Map Your Automation Exposure

List 10 tasks that make up your typical work week. For each one, honestly assess:
- Is this task primarily pattern-following, or does it require contextual judgment?
- Could AI do 80% of this task today? What would the remaining 20% require?
- If AI handles the 80%, what does that free you up to do?

This is not a threat assessment; it is a strategic one. The goal is to identify where you can use AI to multiply your output, and where to invest your irreplaceable human skills.

---

### Exercise 2: Build Your AI News Diet

Set up a simple system to stay current:
1. Subscribe to one AI newsletter
2. Follow one AI researcher or analyst whose perspective you find useful
3. Block 20 minutes every Friday to skim what happened in AI that week

After one month: what did you learn that surprised you? What changed your view?

---

### Exercise 3: Evaluate a Regulatory Claim

Find one article or announcement about AI regulation (EU AI Act, state legislation, or a company's AI policy). Analyze:
- What specific AI applications does it cover?
- What does it require? What does it prohibit?
- Does it affect tools or applications your organization uses or builds?
- What would need to change in your organization to comply?
